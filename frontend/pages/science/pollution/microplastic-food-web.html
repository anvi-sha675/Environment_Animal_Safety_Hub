The Journey of a Plastic Bottle

Click through each stage to see how UV light and wave action break down a single plastic bottle into billions of micro- and nano-particles.

PET
Stage 01

Discarded

A single plastic bottle is carelessly discarded. It enters waterways through storm drains, rivers, or direct littering on beaches. 8 million tons of plastic enter oceans annually.

A plastic bottle takes 450+ years to decompose
Stage 02

UV Photodegradation

Sunlight's UV radiation breaks the polymer chains. The plastic becomes brittle and yellowed, developing surface cracks. This process — photodegradation — is the first step of fragmentation.

UV light breaks C-C and C-H bonds in polymer chains
Stage 03

Mechanical Fragmentation

Waves, wind, and abrasion from sand and rocks physically break the weakened plastic into smaller pieces. What was one bottle becomes thousands of fragments, each less than 5mm.

One bottle can produce over 10,000 microplastic pieces
Stage 04

Microplastics & Nanoplastics

Particles under 5mm are classified as microplastics. Further degradation creates nanoplastics (<1μm) that can penetrate cell membranes. These absorb toxic chemicals like DDT, PCBs, and heavy metals from the water.

Nanoplastics can cross the blood-brain barrier
Stage 05

Entering the Food Web

Zooplankton and filter feeders mistake microplastics for food. These tiny organisms form the base of the marine food web. Every organism that feeds on them now consumes plastic — and the toxins attached to it.

Over 700 marine species are affected by plastic pollution

Apex: Humans

~10,000x base concentration

Through consuming seafood, drinking water, and even breathing air, humans accumulate microplastics from multiple pathways. Studies have found microplastics in human blood, lungs, and placenta.

5g plastic ingested/week
121K particles/year inhaled

Tertiary Consumers: Marine Mammals

~5,000x base concentration

Dolphins, seals, and whales consume large quantities of contaminated fish. Their long lifespans mean decades of plastic accumulation. Necropsies reveal stomachs filled with plastic debris.

100+ particles per gram of tissue
56% of whale species affected

Secondary Consumers: Large Fish

~1,000x base concentration

Tuna, swordfish, and other predatory fish eat hundreds of smaller fish, each carrying their load of microplastics. The toxins — PCBs, mercury, and flame retardants — concentrate in fatty tissues.

1-7 particles per individual
73% of deep-sea fish have microplastics

Primary Consumers: Small Fish & Shellfish

~100x base concentration

Anchovies, sardines, shrimp, and mussels filter enormous volumes of water, ingesting microplastics directly. Mussels can contain up to 90 particles per serving.

1-2 particles per organism
90 particles per mussel serving

Base: Plankton & Zooplankton

1x base concentration

Microscopic organisms absorb nanoplastics directly from the water column. Even at this base level, microplastics disrupt feeding behavior, reduce reproductive rates, and transfer adsorbed pollutants.

Part 4

Breaking the Chain

Toggle each solution to see its impact on the food chain. Systemic changes can make a difference.

0%
Microplastic Reduction

Toggle solutions below to see cumulative impact

Washing Machine Filters

Synthetic clothing releases 700,000 microfibers per wash. External filters capture up to 90% of these fibers.

-35% microfiber release

Banning Microbeads

Cosmetics and personal care products contain plastic microbeads that wash directly into waterways. Bans eliminate this source entirely.

-25% cosmetic microplastics

Advanced Water Treatment

Upgrading municipal water treatment with membrane filtration and advanced oxidation can remove up to 99.9% of microplastics from drinking water.

-20% ingestion via water

Packaging Reform

Replacing single-use plastics with biodegradable alternatives and enforcing Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) policies can reduce plastic entering oceans by 80%.

-30% ocean plastic input

Ocean Cleanup Systems

Passive collection systems in ocean gyres and river interceptors can capture macroplastics before they fragment into microplastics.

-15% ocean surface plastic

Consumer Awareness

Choosing natural-fiber clothing, avoiding single-use plastics, using water filters, and supporting plastic-free brands reduces individual exposure.

-10% personal exposure
